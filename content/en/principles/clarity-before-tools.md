## Observation

A woman's husband fell ill. The doctors offered options — medications, procedures, interventions. She agreed to each one. She was afraid that refusing any help meant she did not love him enough.

Three months later, her husband asked her to stop. He said: *I want to spend what time I have in peace, not in treatment.*

She had been solving the wrong problem. She had been treating her own fear of loss with medicine. The clarity she needed was not about what to do. It was about what her husband actually needed.

## Principle

The Buddha called this *sammā ditthi* — Right View. It is the first step of the Eightfold Path because without it, every action leads somewhere wrong.

We live in a world that rewards doing. We are praised for fixing, acting, moving forward. So when we are uncertain, we reach for tools — for advice, for remedies, for busyness — to feel less helpless. But a tool used without understanding does not solve the problem. It adds to it.

Right View asks: *What is actually happening here?* Not what we fear. Not what others expect. What is true?

This requires stillness. The mind that is already moving cannot see clearly.

## Application

When something feels wrong — in a relationship, in your body, in your household, in your work — resist the first impulse to act.

Sit with the question for one breath. Ask only: *What is actually needed here?*

Not: *What can I do?* Not: *What do others think I should do?* Only: *What is true?*

The answer may be rest. It may be a conversation. It may be letting something go. Whatever it is, it can only be found in stillness first.

A tool used from clarity is a gift. A tool used from fear is noise.
