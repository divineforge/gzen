## Observation

An elderly monk was asked: how do you meditate every morning without fail, even in old age, even when you are tired or unwell?

He said: *I do not ask myself whether I want to. I simply sit.*

He had not been motivated for decades. He had stopped asking the question long ago. The practice no longer depended on a feeling. It depended only on returning to the cushion.

## Principle

The Buddha described *viriya* — right effort, or right energy — as the sixth step of the Eightfold Path. It is not described as enthusiasm, inspiration, or the desire to practice. It is described as the steady application of will: beginning, continuing, not abandoning.

We are taught to wait for the feeling — the energy, the inspiration, the certainty — before we act. But this is backwards. The feeling comes, when it comes, only after we have begun. It is a reward for starting, not a condition for it.

This applies to every good thing we wish to cultivate: patience with our children, kindness to strangers, prayer, care of the body, study. We will not always feel like it. We are not supposed to. The practice asks only that we begin.

## Application

Choose one thing — one small practice — that you have been waiting to feel ready for.

Make the time so small it cannot be refused: five minutes of stillness in the morning, one kind word before the day ends, one page of a book before sleeping.

Remove the decision from the action. Do not ask: *Am I ready?* Ask only: *Did I begin?*

The motivation will come or it will not. Either way, the practice continues. And with continued practice, something quietly changes — not with great feeling, but with great depth.
